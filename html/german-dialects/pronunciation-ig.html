Pronunciations of "ig" in different regions

13th July 2021

There are different dialects throughout the German-speaking countries and of course, different dialects mean different pronunciation rules. I usually teach you about Austrian German, but since this subject has got to do with the Austrian dialects, I thought that it would be interesting to talk with you about that. Today, I want to talk about the suffix “-ig”, which, based on the region, can have 3 different pronunciations:

/iç/ [like “ich”] in northern and in central Germany

/ik/ in southern Germany, Austria and in Switzerland

/iʃ/ [like “isch] in West Middle Germany and in Saxony

The Duden pronunciation dictionary states that the /iç/ pronunciation the correct one is, even though this rule does not apply regularly for the middle of the word. For example, you should say “köniklich” and “ewiklich” but then again “wenichstens”, “zwanzichste” and “ewichkeit”

In the south, there are some regions where you might find the /iç/ pronunciation (Even though it is pretty rare). That is of course due to the spread of Standard German and of mass media. However, the traditionally southern /ik/ pronunciation has also found its way to the north, to Hannover, Hamburg, Berlin etc.. Reasons for that are the inconsequent norm (So is for example the correct pronunciation of “Tag” /taːk/ and not /taç/), but also the orthography, which lets people think that /tak/ would be the correct pronunciation, even though it is not.

As you might have noticed, in the regions of the Rhineland, Hesse and Palatinate, the /ik/ pronunciation is also surprisingly common. This is due to a phenomenon called “hypercorrection”. There, where people try to avoid the /iʃ/ pronunciation, it is much easier to pronounce it as /ik/ instead of /iç/.
