Correction of the "Minor mistakes" form

11th September 2021

Some days ago, I made a survey about some minor mistakes in the German language. (Even though some of them weren’t that minor but never mind!). There were 7 participants who have accomplished an average of 2,4/5 points (48,57%, also note that there is no judgement or anything). So, what are the five mistakes? Let’s start with the first sentence:

  1. Ich hoffe, ihn zu wiedersehen.

This one is a pretty big mistake and mistakes like that are made only by learners. So, what is the correct answer? It is:

  • Ich hoffe, ihn wiederzusehen

RULE: Usually with the “zu + Infinitiv” the “zu” part comes before the infinitive. However, if the infinitive is composed of two words (“wiedersehen” is composed of “wieder” and “sehen”), then the “zu” part comes in between those two words.

Now that we know this rule, let’s go on to the next mistake:

  1. Wie dem auch ist, ich kann da nichts für.

Some of you thought that the separation of “da” and “für” would be wrong, but that’s not the case. In fact, yes, “Ich kann nichts dafür” is the most correct form, however, in colloquial speech, “Ich kann da nichts für” is also totally fine. The real mistake is:

  • Wie dem auch sei, ich kann da nichts für.

RULE: Unfortunately, there is no real rule for that. You just have to remember the phrases in which you have to use the conjunctive I mood.

  1. General De Gaulle leistete in Frankreich Wiederstand gegen das NS-Regime.

This sentence right here is interesting, because this mistake is also common even among natives, even though the grammar point behind it is not that hard. The correct sentence is:

  • General De Gaulle leistete in Frankreich Widerstand gegen das NS-Regime.

RULE: wieder-” means to do something again, it’s like the English “re-” prefix. “wider-” on the other hand means more something like “against, anti-, contra etc.”

  1. Etwas großes kommt auf uns zu.

This mistake right here is pretty common among natives so don’t feel embarrassed if you didn’t get this one right. Even I get that sometimes wrong. The problem here is small. The correct sentence is:

  • Etwas Großes kommt auf uns zu.

RULE: If a declined adjective follows an indefinite quantifier (nichts, viel, wenig, alles, etwas etc.), this adjective is written with a capital letter (same goes for participles btw.)

Last but not least:

  1. Mein Sohn Jonas spielt mit dem roten großen Ball.

Well, I just found out that I messed this one up, the mistake is a different than I had thought. Even though, my original thought:

  • Mein Sohn Jonas spielt mit dem großen roten Ball

Isn’t wrong either. The actual mistake is there needs to be a comma between the two adjectives. So, I won’t count this one, if anyone wants their result to be checked again, please contact me via Discord. You should have got me in your DMs. There is not really a rule about the order of adjectives as there is in English. However, there is still an interesting rule which I want to share with you:

RULE: If two adjectives are equal, that means that they have the same relationship to the adjective, there needs to be a comma.

  • Mein Sohn Jonas spielt mit dem großen, roten Ball.

However, if they are not equal, that means if a fixed relationship between an adjective and a substantive is being more precisely determined by another adjective, there doesn’t need to be a comma, however, the latter needs to stand before the fixed adjective-substantive combination. For example:

  • Er war ein berühmter französischer Schauspieler.

Here is a fixed relationship between “französischer” and “Schauspieler” (it is an undoubtable fact that the actor is French) which is being more precisely determined by “berühmter” (Being a French actor doesn’t make him automatically famous).

So, there you have it, those are the 5 mistakes (4 real mistakes and 1 by my stupidity) I wanted to share with you. I will make something like that again but with mistakes in the range A1-B1 and I will make sure that I don’t make any mistakes by myself.
