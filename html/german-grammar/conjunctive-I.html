Konjunktiv I

Konjunktiv I

(conjunctive I)

29th August 2021

If you should ever be to read a German newspaper or listen to German News on TV or via Podcast, you might come across some verb forms that just seem weird to a learner.

Example: “[…] die Diebe raubten Schmuck und Geld in Wert von über 100.000€. Laut der örtlichen Polizeibehörde handle es sich bei den Tätern um Profis. Es gebe kaum Spuren und auch die Tresortür sei unversehrt. Man arbeite auf Hochtouren an diesem Fall. […]“

As I said earlier, these verb conjugations might seem strange or even wrong to people who aren’t familiar with the Konjunktiv I.

Like any other Indo-European language, German has got so-called moods, i.e., the conjugation might change based on the speaker’s feelings about the subject. Standard German knows 5 moods:

  • Infinitiv
  • Indikativ
  • Imperativ
  • Konjunktiv I
  • Konjunktiv II

Today however, we will just concentrate on the Konjunktiv I mood. This mood is used:

  • For reported speech (See the example from earlier)
  • In certain sayings like “Es lebe der König! “ (In this case, it is somewhat used as a substitute for the 3rd Person Singular imperative which doesn’t exist)

In the case of the reported speech, the Konjunktiv I is only used in writing or by newspapers. In everyday speech however, people tend to use the indicative form.

Example: Er sagte mir, dass er gestern Fußball gespielt habe.

In everyday speech, people just say:

Er sagte mir, dass er gestern Fußball gespielt hat.

(Of course, when speaking in reported speech, keep in mind to change the adverbs of place and time if needed)

Example: Er sagte: „Sie war gestern hier “

Er sagte, dass sie am Tag zuvor dort gewesen sei.

So, why doesn’t the media use the indicative mood as well? That’s because the indicative implies that you believe the person who told what you report and hence people are more likely to take that information as a fact. With the Konjunktiv I however, the media wants to keep a bit of neutrality, it doesn't seem as much like a fact anymore.

The Konjunktiv I mood only exists for 4 out of the 6 German tenses:

  • Präsens
  • Perfekt
  • Futur I
  • Futur II

Now that we know when to use the Konjunktiv I, we need to know how to conjugate it

Conjugation of the Konjunktiv I

To conjugate a verb into the Konjunktiv I, we have to take the verb stem and add the following endings to it:

ich -e wir -en
du -est ihr -et
er/sie/es -e sie -en

Example for the word “gehen”:

ich gehe wir gehen
du gehest ihr gehet
er/sie/es gehe sie gehen

Easy isn’t it? Not in practice, because there are many exceptions. The only verb where the Konjunktiv I forms look like this is the verb “sein”

ich sei wir seien
du seiest ihr seiet
er/sie/es sei sie seien

For the other verbs, the Konjunktiv I has only got its pure form for the 3rd Person Singular. Otherwise:

  • For the 2nd Person both Singular and Plural, the Konjunktiv I is very similar to the indicative, hence it is possible to conjugate them like that, but to make the distinction between the conjunctive and the indicative clearer, the Konjunktiv II form is preferred.
  • For the 1st Person both Singular and Plural, as well for the 3rd Person Plural, the Konjunktiv I form is equivalent to the indicative form. Hence in this case, the Konjunktiv II form actually has to be used if you want to keep the uncertainty in your statement.

So, the real table for the verb “gehen”, would be:

ich ginge wir gingen
du gehest/gingest ihr gehet/ginget
er/sie/es gehe sie gingen

When using the perfect tense, the modal verb “sein” or “haben” is put in the Konjunktiv I form, the other part of the verb is in the Partizip II form as usual.

ich sei gegangen wir seien gegangen
du seist gegangen ihr seiet gegangen
er/sie/es sei gegangen sie seien gegangen

For the future I and II, the modal verb “werden” is put into the Konjunktiv I/II form, the rest is conjugated as usual.

ich würde gehen

ich würde gegangen sein

wir würden gehen

wir würden gegangen sein

du werdest gehen

du werdest gegangen sein

ihr würdet gehen

ihr würdet gegangen sein

er/sie/es werde gehen

er/sie/es werde gegangen sein

sie würden gehen

sie würden gegangen sein
