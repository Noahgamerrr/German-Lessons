German cases

8th March 2021

One of the aspects of German grammar that many people wrap their head around is the case system. It is notoriously difficult for people, whose native language doesn't distinguish nouns by their function in the sentence. Unfortunately, there is no clear rule that easily explains when to use which case. I will still try to explain this part of grammar the best, so that you can guess more easily which case might be correct in which context.

German, as most of you know, has got 4 cases:

  • nominative
  • genitive
  • dative
  • accusative

The declension mainly affects the articles of German nouns.

The German declension can be divided into 5 classes, which are named after the nominative plural:

Now that we know what the declension looks like, we are going to look at when to use which case in itself.

Nominative

The nominative case is the most self-explaining case of all: It is used, when the noun is the subject i.e. the main actor of the sentence. It is also the noun that is usually refered to first in the main clause. So there isn't really any explanation needed for this case.

Genitive

The genitive case is used, when a noun is in relationship with another noun.

Example: Das Auto des Mannes ist grün.

(The man's car is green)

It is also usually used after the prepositions:

während, wegen, trotz, innerhalb, außerhalb, oberhalb, unterhalb, aufgrund, anstelle, (an)statt

But also after specific verbs:

Einer Sache: gedenken, bedürfen, Herr werden

Jemanden einer Sache: anklagen, belangen, bezichtigen, überführen

Sich einer Sache: bewusst sein, brüsten, erinnern, erfreuen, enthalten, schämen

That might look intimidating, but luckily, the genitive case gets used less and less by native speakers.

Dative

The dative is known as the indirect object. It is used after the verbs like:

helfen, gehören, zuhören

But also after prepositions like:

aus, bei, gegenüber, mit, nach, seit, von and zu

Fun Fact: The dative object usually receives the accusative object from the subject

Example: Ich gab dem Mann das Buch.

(I gave the book to the man)

Accusative

The accusative is also know as the direct object and often is the thing that is being received by the dative object. It is used after most verbs, and after the prepositions:

um, durch, ohne, bis, für, gegen
