Usage of tenses

25th December 2021

Like any other Indo-European language, German has got a tense system. Today, we want to take a closer look at them and see, which ones are used more frequently in everyday speech, and which aren't.

German itself knows six tenses:

  • Präsens
  • Perfekt
  • Präteritum
  • Plusquamperfekt
  • Futur I
  • Futur II

In this lesson, we will take a look at how they are used, in formal speech, as well as in everyday speech.

Präsens

The German present tense is very frequently used. It's usage ranges from the past, to present, till the future. Its usage involves:

  • Things that are happening in the present

    Example: Er spielt nun Fußball. (He is now playing football)

  • Things that happen frequently

    Example: Ich esse jede Woche Nudeln. (I eat pasta every week)

  • Things that indicate how long an action is ongoing

    Example: Ich spiele seit 90 Minuten Schach. (I have been playing chess for 90 minutes)

  • Things that are planned for the future (With a time indication)

    Example: Morgen Abend gehen wir spazieren. (We are going for a walk tomorrow evening)

  • (In writing) Historical Present as a way of telling a story.

    Example: Im Jahre 1799 übernimmt Napoleon die Macht in Frankreich. (In 1799, Napoleon takes over the power in France.)

Perfekt & Präteritum

Both the perfect and the preterit tense in German are used for actions that happened in the past. The only difference between those two is, that the perfect tense is used in everyday speech, while the preterit is rather to be found in writing.

Example: (colloquial) Ich habe gestern Fußball gespielt.

(Writing:) Ich spielte gestern Fußball.

(Yesterday, I played football.)

Plusquamperfekt

The pluperfect is used for actions that had happened before another action happened in the past. However, this tense can only be found in writing.

Example: Bevor wir alle am Tisch saßen und aßen, hatte ich mit meinem Bruder ein Bild gemalt.

Before we all sat around the table and ate, I had painted an image with my brother.

In colloquial speech, however, it is rather dying. Instead, people tend to use the perfect tense

Example: Bevor wir alle am Tisch saßen und aßen, habe ich mit meinem Bruder ein Bild gemalt.

In specific regions like in Bavaria or in Austria, there is something called the "doppeltes Perfekt". Which is used instead of the pluperfect. (There will be a lesson soon where I talk about that in more detail).

Example: Bevor wir alle am Tisch saßen und aßen, habe ich mit meinem Bruder ein Bild gemalt gehabt.

Futur I

The future I, contrary to its name, isn't always used for the future. Instead, people tend to use the present. The only time people use the future I with the future is when an action will have a huge impact on the future, or if there is no time indication.

Example: Im nächsten Jahr lerne ich Deutsch.

Next year, I'm going to learn German.

Example: In 30 Jahren werden wir kein Erdöl mehr haben.

In 30 years, we won't have any crude oil anymore.

Other usages for the future I include:

  • Predictions about the present.

    Example: Sein Zimmer wird schon sauber sein. (His room will be cleaned up.)

  • Predictions about the future.

    Example: Das wirst du an bis dahin nicht schaffen. (Till then, you won't be finished)

Futur II

Last, but not least, the future II tense. It is somewhat common in colloquial speech, but some people tend to use the perfect tense instead. So:

Example: Er wird sein Zimmer schon aufgeräumt haben.

He will have cleaned his room.

Becomes:

Example: Er hat sein Zimmer schon aufgeräumt.

If you want to use the future II, then here are the most common use cases:

  • Predictions about the past.

    Example: Er wird sein Zimmer schon aufgeräumt haben. (He will have cleaned his room.)

  • Predictions about what will have happened in the future in a specific time (With time indication).

    Example: Bis morgen werde ich das Buch fertiggelesen haben. (Tomorrow, I will have finished reading this book.)
